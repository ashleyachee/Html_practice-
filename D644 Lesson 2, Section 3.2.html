Free Speech & Commercial Speech

What is it?

The First Amendment protects many forms of expression, including commercial speech such as advertising. However, commercial speech can be regulated to prevent misleading or deceptive claims.

Why it matters

Organizations can promote products and ideas, but advertising must be truthful and should not mislead consumers.

Example

A company may advertise a product's benefits, but it cannot make false claims about what the product can do.

Defamation: Libel and Slander

What is it?

Defamation is a false statement communicated to someone else that harms a person's or organization's reputation.

Key distinction
  • Libel: written or published defamatory communication
  • Slander: spoken defamatory communication
Example

Publishing a false claim that a competitor committed fraud could damage its reputation and create legal risk.

Deception

What is it?

Deception involves false or misleading communication, including false advertising, misleading statements, or marketing tactics that distort the truth.

Why it matters

Deceptive communication undermines trust and may violate consumer protection laws, leading to fines, lawsuits, or reputational harm.

Example

An advertisement that says “unlimited service” while hiding strict usage limits may mislead consumers.

CAN-SPAM

What is it?

The CAN-SPAM Act is a U.S. law that regulates commercial email communication.

Key requirements
  • Use accurate sender information
  • Avoid deceptive subject lines or headers
  • Provide a clear way to unsubscribe
Example

A promotional email should clearly identify the sender and include a working unsubscribe option.

General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

What is it?

The GDPR is the European Union's data privacy regulation. It applies to organizations that process the personal data of EU residents, even if the organization is located outside the EU.

Why it matters

Organizations must handle personal data responsibly, including how data is collected, used, protected, and supported by consent where required.

Example

A website may need to explain how it uses personal data and ask for consent before collecting certain information.

Key takeaway

Free speech is important, but professional communication also has legal and ethical limits. Defamation laws, commercial speech rules, CAN-SPAM, and GDPR help protect reputations, consumers, and personal privacy.
