Lean vs. Six Sigma

This interactive infographic introduces two common improvement methodologies at a high level. Click each section to see what each approach focuses on, how it helps organizations, and where the two overlap.

Big picture

Lean and Six Sigma are both used to improve organizational performance, but they emphasize different problems. Lean focuses on eliminating waste and improving flow. Six Sigma focuses on reducing errors, variation, and defects.

Lean

Move work faster and remove activities that do not add value.

Six Sigma

Make processes more consistent and reduce mistakes.

Both

Improve quality, efficiency, and decision-making.

Simple way to remember it

Lean asks: “How can we remove waste?”

Six Sigma asks: “How can we reduce defects and variation?”

Together they support: better processes, better customer outcomes, and more effective operations.

L

Lean

Focuses on reducing waste and improving the flow of work.
Core idea:

Lean is about identifying steps, time, or resources that do not add value for the customer and finding ways to streamline the process.

Main focus

  • Removing waste
  • Improving speed and flow
  • Making work more efficient

High-level example

If a process includes extra handoffs, waiting time, or unnecessary steps, Lean looks for ways to simplify it.

Helpful question

Which parts of this process are slowing us down without helping the customer?

6σ

Six Sigma

Focuses on reducing variation, defects, and inconsistency in a process.
Core idea:

Six Sigma is about making processes more stable, predictable, and accurate so organizations can reduce errors and improve quality.

Main focus

  • Reducing errors
  • Reducing variation
  • Improving consistency and quality

High-level example

If the same process produces different outcomes each time, Six Sigma works to find the cause and make results more reliable.

Helpful question

Why are results inconsistent, and how can we make the process more dependable?

+

What they share

Both aim to improve processes and create better outcomes for customers and organizations.
Shared purpose:

Both methodologies support continuous improvement. They help organizations become more intentional about how work gets done.

Shared goals

  • Better quality
  • Better efficiency
  • Better customer experience

Shared mindset

Both encourage teams to study processes, identify problems, and make informed improvements rather than guessing.

Helpful question

How can we improve the way this work is done so results are stronger and more consistent?

?

When to use each approach

Choose the focus based on the type of problem the organization is trying to solve.
Simple rule:

Use Lean when the problem is waste or slow flow. Use Six Sigma when the problem is inconsistency or defects. Many organizations blend both.

Lean fits when...

  • Work takes too long
  • There are too many extra steps
  • Customers are waiting unnecessarily

Six Sigma fits when...

  • Errors happen too often
  • Results vary from case to case
  • Quality is not consistent

Using both

An organization may streamline a process with Lean and then improve consistency with Six Sigma.

At a glance

  • Lean: remove waste and improve flow.
  • Six Sigma: reduce defects and variation.
  • Both: improve processes and support better results.

Student takeaway

You do not need to memorize advanced tools to understand the difference. At a high level, Lean is about efficiency, while Six Sigma is about consistency and quality.

Main takeaway

Lean helps organizations do work with less waste. Six Sigma helps organizations do work with fewer errors. Together, they provide a strong foundation for process improvement.
