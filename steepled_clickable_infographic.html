What is a STEEPLED analysis?

A STEEPLED analysis is a framework for scanning the external environment. It helps managers look beyond the company itself and identify outside forces that may create opportunities, risks, or pressure for change.

  • 1
    Scan the environment
    Look at major trends happening outside the organization.
  • 2
    Interpret business impact
    Decide how those trends could affect customers, operations, or strategy.
  • 3
    Support decisions
    Use your findings to guide planning, innovation, and risk management.

What does STEEPLED stand for?

S = Social
T = Technological
E = Economic
E = Environmental
P = Political
L = Legal
E = Ethical
D = Demographic

S

Social

How people’s values, lifestyles, attitudes, and behaviors influence business.
Core idea:

Social forces reflect how people think, live, work, and interact. These trends can shape demand, brand expectations, workplace culture, and consumer preferences.

What to look for

  • Changing attitudes and values
  • Work-life balance expectations
  • Consumer lifestyle trends
  • Education and social norms

Simple example

A company may offer flexible services or healthier product options because customers value convenience and well-being.

Ask this

How are changes in values or behavior affecting what customers expect from organizations?

T

Technological

How innovation, digital tools, and new systems affect competition and operations.
Core idea:

Technology can change how organizations create value, communicate, produce, analyze data, and serve customers.

What to look for

  • Automation and AI
  • Platform and software changes
  • Cybersecurity concerns
  • Communication technologies

Simple example

A retailer may invest in chatbots, data dashboards, or e-commerce tools to improve customer service and efficiency.

Ask this

What emerging technologies could improve performance or disrupt the current business model?

E

Economic

How financial conditions influence spending, costs, investment, and growth.
Core idea:

Economic conditions affect purchasing power, borrowing, hiring, pricing, and organizational stability.

What to look for

  • Inflation and interest rates
  • Employment levels
  • Consumer confidence
  • Supply and cost pressures

Simple example

During inflation, a business may face higher material costs while customers become more price-sensitive.

Ask this

How might economic conditions change customer demand, operating costs, or strategic priorities?

E

Environmental

How natural systems and sustainability pressures influence business choices.
Core idea:

Environmental factors include climate issues, resource use, waste, sustainability expectations, and physical environmental risks.

What to look for

  • Climate-related disruptions
  • Energy and resource usage
  • Waste reduction expectations
  • Sustainability initiatives

Simple example

A manufacturer may redesign packaging to reduce waste and respond to customer interest in sustainable practices.

Ask this

What environmental pressures could affect supply chains, reputation, or long-term planning?

P

Political

How government priorities and public policy can shape the business environment.
Core idea:

Political forces include government actions, policy agendas, international relations, taxation, and funding priorities.

What to look for

  • Policy shifts
  • Trade conditions
  • Public funding priorities
  • Political stability

Simple example

A company in healthcare or energy may adjust strategy based on changes in government priorities or funding.

Ask this

Are political decisions creating new risks, restrictions, or opportunities for the organization?

L

Legal

How laws and regulations affect what organizations must or must not do.
Core idea:

Legal factors focus on formal rules. These can shape hiring, contracts, data use, product standards, advertising, and industry compliance.

What to look for

  • Labor and employment law
  • Privacy and data regulation
  • Consumer protection rules
  • Industry-specific compliance

Simple example

A business may change its customer data practices to comply with stronger privacy requirements.

Ask this

What legal requirements could raise costs, require process changes, or increase risk exposure?

E

Ethical

How expectations about fairness, responsibility, and integrity influence decisions.
Core idea:

Ethical factors are not only about legality. They involve doing what is responsible, fair, and trustworthy for stakeholders.

What to look for

  • Fairness and transparency
  • Responsible sourcing
  • Bias in decision-making
  • Stakeholder trust

Simple example

An organization might avoid a profitable tactic if it feels misleading, exploitative, or harmful to its customers.

Ask this

Even if something is legal, would stakeholders see it as fair, responsible, and aligned with the company’s values?

D

Demographic

How population characteristics can affect markets, labor, and service needs.
Core idea:

Demographic factors examine who people are in measurable terms, such as age, income, education, location, and population change.

What to look for

  • Population growth or decline
  • Age distribution
  • Income levels
  • Geographic migration patterns

Simple example

A growing older population may increase demand for healthcare services, financial planning, or accessibility-focused products.

Ask this

How might changes in population characteristics alter customer needs or workforce availability?

How students can use STEEPLED

  1. Choose an organization, industry, or issue.
  2. Review each STEEPLED category one at a time.
  3. Identify 1-3 external trends that matter most.
  4. Explain whether each trend creates an opportunity, a threat, or both.
  5. Use the analysis to support a recommendation or strategic response.

Helpful reminder

STEEPLED is not a list of internal company problems. It is a way to examine the outside environment. The goal is to understand what is happening around the organization and why it matters.

Strong analyses do more than name a trend. They connect the trend to business impact.

Main takeaway

STEEPLED helps managers think broadly. When leaders pay attention to social, technological, economic, environmental, political, legal, ethical, and demographic forces, they are better prepared to make informed and strategic decisions.
